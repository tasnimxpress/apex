One Connected Pattern.

The money we manage is growing — but so is the money leaking out. Beneath the headline numbers, four problems feed each other: one fund is shrinking (paying out more than it takes in), our biggest fund signs up lots of customers but keeps too few, 68% of our money sits with the top 10% of customers who get no special care, and Relationship Managers are rewarded for signing customers up but not for keeping them. Together this is a built-in leak, not a passing market dip.

What Management Should Do
  • Change how Relationship Managers are paid, now: Pay the full sign-up bonus only if the customer stays a year — no full bonus on accounts that close within 12 months. This single change is the most powerful lever management has.
  • Write down Sonia Sheikh's approach: Her sign-up call script, how she picks the right fund for each customer, and her 90-day follow-up routine. Share it with all 16 Relationship Managers through the Sales Manager within 30 days.
  • Add "customers kept" to the monthly review: Right now the only Relationship Manager number reviewed each month is how much they bring in. Adding how many customers they keep for a year makes the quality-vs-quantity trade-off visible — and something we can act on.
  • Give Capital Growth to the best Relationship Managers: New Capital Growth accounts should go first to Relationship Managers who keep more than 88 of every 100 customers a year, until the Relationship Managers we're watching show real improvement.
  • Decide: chase volume or value. The average first investment has roughly halved since 2018 (৳2.85 lakh → ৳0.97 lakh) even as sign-ups soared. If the goal is bigger, stickier customers, set a minimum-ticket or higher-value target — today's growth is mostly small accounts.
Two Kinds of Relationship Manager Need Two Different Plans
Relationship Managers of our biggest customers
• Give them fewer customers each, so every big customer gets real attention.
• Reward them for keeping these customers, not just for finding new ones.
• Have them call before each dividend, so big customers reinvest instead of cashing out.
Relationship Managers of many small customers
• Pay the sign-up bonus only after the customer has stayed a full year.
• Check every new account within 3 months to be sure the fund suited the customer.
• Move the smallest accounts to phone and app self-service, freeing Relationship Manager time for customers who can grow.
Sign-up volume is strong — 2,616 new accounts in 2025, the most ever. But each new customer now brings in less than half what they did in 2018. Limits of the Data
  • No market-price data: All money figures are based on cash paid in and taken out, not investment returns. So "money growth" shows how much of customers' money the fund held onto, not how well it invested.
  • Paying on time isn't the same as staying: Keeping up payments measures whether customers pay regularly. A customer can pay every month and still close their account — so don't read it as customers kept.
  • How we identify a customer: We use the mobile number as the customer's ID, as the brief required. Shared or changed numbers may throw the count off by 1–3%.
  • Which Relationship Manager gets credit: Based on the Relationship Manager who opened the account. If an account was later moved, the original Relationship Manager still gets credit — so a few Relationship Managers' current quality may look lower than it is.
  • How we count a lost customer: Closed or stopped accounts only. Merely inactive accounts aren't counted, and 2 suspended accounts are left out entirely.
  • 2026 is a part-year: Data runs through May 2026 (~5 months), so all 2026 totals are lower than the full year will be. 2025 is the latest complete year.
How the Analysis Was Done
  • Grouping customers: Customers were sorted into 4 look-alike groups by balance, monthly amount, age, and plan length. We tested different numbers of groups and 4 gave the clearest split.
  • How long accounts last: We tracked when accounts close and measured how many are still active at 1, 2, and 3 years, keeping still-open accounts in the count until the data ends.
  • The overall fund grade: A blend of how few customers we lose (35%), how much customers' money grew (30%), still active at 3 years (20%), and payments kept up (15%), put on a 0–100 scale. It compares the four funds against each other, not against an outside standard.
